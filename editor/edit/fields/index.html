Symbiota Data Fields

This page describes the fields available in Symbiota. The Symbiota data schema is strongly aligned to the Darwin Core data exchange standard. For more details, links to the Darwin Core definitions are supplied for each term.

Since portals have the ability to customize the field names found on their data entry form, field names may differ from the core field definition and how it is mapped to Darwin Core export tools.

Standard Fields

FieldDescriptionExample(s)Darwin Core field
Catalog NumberThe unique identifier (primary key) for the specimen record. This field should be used to store the barcode or the accession number (herbaria only). This field is enforced to be unique per collection.WIS-L-0123456, ASU0012345, 12345catalogNumber
Other Catalog NumbersAny other identifier for a specimen record that is not the catalog number. This field is typically used to store the old catalog number for collections that are in the process of switching from one cataloging system to another (e.g. barcode system).12345otherCatalogNumbers
CollectorThe name of the person who collected the specimen or made the observation.C.G. Pringle, Goodding, L.N.recordedBy
Associated CollectorsOther collectors that were present at the time of collectionJohn R. Reeder, A. NelsonThis field is not defined by the Darwin Core standard, which places primary and secondary collectors concatenated the recordedBy field.
NumberThe collection number assigned to the specimen by the collector.1294, 12490b, 94-132recordNumber
DateThe date the specimen was collected. While dates can be entered using your preferred format, the value will be converted and stored as an ISO-8601 numeric format (YYYY-MM-DD). Note that unknown month and days can be entered as “00”. For example, a collection with a date of “March 1956” can be entered as “1956-03-00”.1983-09-15, 1983-07-00, 1934-00-00eventDate
Verbatim DateCan be used to record date exactly as entered on label. Particularly useful for non-standard date formats or date ranges.Spring 1901, March-April 1952, late Sept. 1909verbatimEventDate
Year, Month, DayThe numeric value of the year, month, and day at the time of collection. These fields are automatically populated when the date is entered.year, month, day
Day of year rangeA range of collection dates can be represented here as numeric day of year values. These values will be automatically calculated if you enter a date range in the verbatim date field12 Sept 1968 to 19 Sept 1968, 1968-09-12 to 1968-09-19)startDayOfYear, endDayOfYear
Scientific NameThe Latin name of the specimen without the author. Could be anything from kingdom down to subspecies or variety, depending on the level of the identification.Pinaceae, Pinus, Pinus edulis, Pinus edulis var. fallaxscientificName
AuthorThe name of the person who first named the taxon. This field autofills after entering the scientific name as long as that taxon is in the taxonomic thesaurus.L., Asa A. GrayscientificNameAuthorship
Identification QualifierThe determiner’s expression of uncertainty in their identification. This will be listed on the label along with the scientific name.cf., aff.identificationQualifier
FamilyThe family to which the taxa belongs. This field autofills after entering the scientific name as long as the taxon exists in the taxonomic thesaurus.Pinaceae, Asteraceaefamily
Identified ByThe name of the person who identified the specimen. Also called a determiner.L. R. LandrumidentifiedBy
Date IdentifiedThe date the identification was made. Date can be entered as free form text and do not need to be in a standard date format.1992, May 1992, 2 May 1992dateIdentified
ID ReferencesThe reference source used to make the identification.Nesom, Guy L. 2006. Flora of North America – Asteraceae. vol. 20identificationReferences
ID RemarksAny additional notes regarding the identification of the specimen.identificationRemarks
Taxon RemarksAny additional notes regarding the taxonomic name to which the specimen was identify.taxonRemarks
CountryThe name of the country in which the specimen was collected. To aid data entry, a drop down menu will appear as one types, though names outside of the list can still be entered.United States, Canada, Mexicocountry
State/ProvinceThe name of the state or province in which the specimen was collected. As one types, a selection list will appear for the given country.New York, Arizona, SonorastateProvince
CountyThe name of the county in which the specimen was collected. Choose one from the drop down menu. For specimens collected outside of the United States, enter the next geographic region below state/province.Maricopacounty
MunicipalityThe name of the municipality in which the specimen was collected. For specimens collected outside of the United States, enter the 4th level geographic designation.Paradise Valley, Tucsonmunicipality
LocalityThe detailed location in which the specimen was collected.9.5 miles NW of Sedona along Boynton Pass Rd.Locality
Location RemarksComments or notes about the Location.under water since 2005locationRemarks
Location IDAn identifier for the set of location information (data associated with dcterms:Location). May be a global unique identifier or an identifier specific to the data set.https://opencontext.org/subjects/ 768A875F-E205-4D0B-DE55-BAB7598D0FD1locationID
Locality SecurityChecking the Locality Security checkbox will hide images and locality details below the level of county from unauthorized users. For more information about locality security in Symbiota, visit our Redacting Data page.This field is not defined by the Darwin Core standard.
Latitude and LongitudeThe geographic latitude and longitude in decimal degrees. Latitudes from the southern hemisphere and longitudes in the western hemisphere (e.g. USA) should be entered as negative values. Click on the “Tools” button to enter the coordinates in the degree, minute, seconds (DMS) or the UTM formats. Decimal degrees are the preferred coordinate standard as defined by Darwin Core.34.874022, -111.75774decimalLatitude, decimalLongitude
Uncertainty (meters)The accuracy of the georeference coordinates in meters (numeric value only). This is measured as the radius of a circle where the true point would be found if known. Protocols for assigning error radii to georeference points can be found in this Georeference Quick Reference Guide.1500, 10000coordinateUncertaintyInMeters
DatumThe geographic system that was used to get the coordinates. This field autofills when using GeoLocate or the Google Maps tool for georeferencing.NAD27, NAD83, WGS84geodeticDatum
Verbatim CoordinatesIf the coordinates recorded on the specimen label are in a format other than decimal degrees, enter them here. Click the F (format) button to enter non-decimal formats (e.g., UTMs, degrees/minutes/seconds) and click Insert Coordinates. This will place the original format in Verbatim Coordinates and will auto-convert to decimal degrees in the Latitude and Longitude fields if these fields are blank. Click the “«” symbol to replace existing decimal values.34° 13.940′ N 112° 2.370′ W, 34d 13m 12.940s N 112d 20m 46.370s W, 12 420944E 4064025N, TRS: T40N R32E S29verbatimCoordinates
Elevation in MetersThe elevation in meters at which the specimen was collected. Also called altitude. Use only the left field with the right field blank when a single elevation exists.1400, 2000-2200minimumElevationInMeters, maximumElevationInMeters
Verbatim ElevationThe verbatim elevation at which the specimen was collected. This is typically used to record an elevation measurement that was recorded in feet or an uncertainty designation. When the elevation in meters field is left blank, the value will automatically be converted to meters if you press Tab after entering a value in this field. Click the “«” symbol to replace the previously entered meters values.4500ft, 4500 feet, ca 4500′, ca 2000m, 4500′ +-300′verbatimElevation
Depth in MetersThe range of depth below the local surface, in meters.100, 150-200minimumDepthInMeters, maximumDepthInMeters
Verbatim DepthThe original verbatim description of the depth below the local surface at which the specimen was collected.100ft, 100 feet, ca 100′, ca 30m, 100′ +-10′verbatimDepth
Georeferenced ByThe name of the person who georeferenced the specimen record. This field autofills when using GeoLocate for georeferencing.A. Gonzales, emakings, acbarbergeoreferencedBy
Georeference ProtocolThe source of the standards used to georeference.Georeferencing Quick Guide. Zermoglio et al. 2020georeferenceProtocol
Georeference SourcesThe tool or tools used to georeference.GeoLocate, Google Earth, USGS mapgeoreferenceSources
Georef Verificiation StatusIndicates whether or not the georeference has been reviewed or verified.reviewed, not reviewedgeoreferenceVerificationStatus
Georeference RemarksAny additional notes regarding the georeferencing of the specimen.georeferenceRemarks
HabitatThe description of the habitat in which the specimen was collected.Wet areas along a small stream in chaparral, roadsidehabitat
SubstrateThe substrate on which the specimen was collected. Mostly used for lichen and bryophyte specimens.On basalt, trunk of oakDarwin Core lumps this information into habitat.
Associated TaxaA list of the names of other species occurring with the collected specimen.Quercus, Arctostaphylos, Ceanothus, Rhus, Eriogonum, SalviaassociatedTaxa
DescriptionA physical description of the specimen at the time of collection. This often includes information that can be lost or difficult to observe after the collection and preservation process.Shrub 3 m tall, corolla yellowIn a Darwin Core export, this is concatenated with occurrenceRemarks
NotesAny additional notes regarding the specimen. This field often includes characteristics of the population, such as its frequency or rarity.occurrenceRemarks
Dynamic PropertiesA list of additional measurements, facts, characteristics, or assertions about the specimen in a format that allows programmatic parsing of the dataawnLengthInMeters=0.014, heightInMeters=1.5, relativeHumidity=28, airTemperatureInC=22dynamicProperties
Life StageThe age or stage of the organism at the time of collection/observation. Typically used for zoological collections.larva, juvenilelifeStage
SexThe biological sex of the occurrence.female, malesex
Individual CountThe number of individuals in the occurrence0, 2, 15individualCount
Sampling ProtocolThe names and references to methods used to collect or sample an occurrenceUV light trap, mist net, Takats et al. 2001. Guidelines for Nocturnal Owl Monitoring in North America. Beaverhill Bird Observatory and Bird Studies Canada, Edmonton, Alberta. 32 pp., http://www.bsc-eoc.org/download/Owl.pdfsamplingProtocol
PreparationsPreparation or preservation method for a specimenin ethanol, skeletonpreparations
Phenology (Reproductive Condition)The reproductive stage the specimen is in. Typically used for plant and fungal collections.flower, fruit, sterilereproductiveCondition
Establishment MeansThe state of establishment at the time of collection.cultivated, invasive, nativeestablishmentMeans
Cultivated CheckboxCheck when the organism was established with the aid of humans and would not be able to exist on their own. This true/false field enables the ability to filter non-native or naturalized species.Not currently exported in DwC format, but see degreeOfEstablishment
Type StatusThe type designation of a specimen, if it is a type specimenHOLOTYPE, ISOTYPE, PARATYPEtypeStatus
DispositionThe location or status of the physical specimen.missing, on loan, cone collectiondisposition
Occurrence IdThis is the Global Unique Identification (GUID) for the specimen. This identification code should be stable and uniquely identify the specimen relative to all other specimens within the world.000866d2-c177-4648-a200-ead4007051b9, urn:catalog:UWBM:Bird:89776occurrenceID
Field NumberfieldNumber
Owner CodeThe acronym of the owning institution. Only enter a value if the owning institution is different than what was entered when the metadata for the collection institution was added to the portal.NPS, Forest ServiceownerInstitutionCode
Basis of RecordThe type of record the specimen is classified as. For physical collections, this field defaults to “PreservedSpecimen” (aka herbarium specimen) and for observation projects, the default is “Observation”.PreservedSpecimen, LivingSpecimen, ObservationbasisOfRecord
LanguageThe language of the label information.English, Spanish, Portugueselanguage
Processing StatusThe status of the digital record. This field is used for internal data management and review. The values used are dictated by the specific workflow of each institution.Expert Required, Pending Review
Label ProjectUsed for printing labels. You can create a label project and print that set of labels after you’ve entered the data.Plants of Sedona 2012
Duplicate QuantityThe number of duplicate specimens created. This will dictate the number of labels printed for specimen.10

Paleo Terms

FieldDescriptionExample(s)Darwin Core field
EonThe longest geologic time intervals.Archean, Proterozoic, PhanerozoicearliestEonOrLowestEonothem, latestEonOrHighestEonothem
EraA subdivision of an eon that is a shorter interval of geologic time.Paleozoic, Mesozoic, CenozoicearliestEraOrLowestErathem, latestEraOrHighestErathem
PeriodA subdivision of an era that is a shorter interval of geologic time.Ordovician, Silurian, Devonian, Carboniferous, Mississippian, Pennsylvanian, Permian, Triassic, Jurassic, Cretaceous, Paleogene, Neogene, Quaternary.earliestPeriodOrLowestSystem, latestPeriodOrHighestSystem
EpochA subdivision of a period that is a shorter interval of geologic time. The Epoch field is currently being recorded using Series, the chronostratigraphic units.Lower, Middle, Upper Ordovician; WenlockearliestEpochOrLowestSeries, latestEpochOrHighestSeries
StageThe chronostratigraphic term given to the succession of rock strata laid down in a single geochronologic age.Lochkovian, Emsian, Eifelian, Givetian, FrasnianearliestAgeOrLowestStage, latestAgeOrHighestStage
Local StageA local name for a stage that was applied to this specimen.“Ulatsian”, “Helvetian.”
Early IntervalName of the earliest possible geochronologic eon, era, period, epoch or age, or the lowest chronostratigraphic eonothem, erathem, system, series, or stage attributable to the stratigraphic horizon from which the cataloged specimen was collected.Aalenian, Aeronian, Albian, Anisian
Late IntervalName of the latest possible geochronologic eon, era, period, epoch or age, or the highest chronostratigraphic eonothem, erathem, system, series or stage attributable to the stratigraphic horizon from which the cataloged specimen was collected.Aalenian, Aeronian, Albian, Anisian
Absolute AgeAge of specimen/rock in years determined using radioactive decay of isotopes (Carbon-14, argon-argon, potassium-argon, uranium-lead, etc.) and other quantitative dating methods.20 Ma, 75 ka, 10.5 – 12.7 +/- 0.5 Ma
Storage AgeField for institutions that arrange collections by geologic time or biostratigraphy. The physical location of a specimen within the collection space.Wasatchian, Paleocene, Bridgerian
Biota (Flora/Fauna)Name given to collections of fossils of the same age from a single locality or multiple localities within a specific geographic area.Chalk Bluffs, Stewart Valley, Bridge Creek, Mazon Creek
Biostratigraphy (Biozone)The name of the lowest possible geological biostratigraphic zone of the stratigraphic horizon from which the cataloged item was collected.Wa0, Uvigerinella sparsicostata Zone, OgygiocarislowestBiostratigraphicZone, highestBiostratigraphicZone
GroupThe name of the lithostratigraphic group from which the cataloged specimen was collected.Bathurst, Lower Wealden, Monte Cristo, Contra Costa, Panochegroup
FormationThe name of the lithostratigraphic formation from which the cataloged specimen was collected. The National Geologic Map Database Geolex Search is a great resource for the named lithostratigraphic units accepted by the USGS.Notch Peak, House Limestone, Fillmore, Chinleformation
MemberThe name of the lithostratigraphic member from which the cataloged item was collected.Lava Dam, Hellnmaria, Brown Mountain Sandstonemember
BedThe name of the lithostratigraphic bed from which the cataloged item was collected.Harlem coalbed
Taxon EnvironmentThe depositional environment of the rock unit from which cataloged specimen was collected.marine, lacustrine, non-marine, marine-non-marine
LithologyField for terms to describe the types of rock/sediment from which the cataloged specimen was collected.sandstone, mudstone, siltstone, shalelithostratigraphicTerms
Strat RemarksField to record additional details about the geology, stratigraphy, more detailed lithology description, palynological sampling info, core data, etc.
ElementField to record type of plant organ cataloged specimen represents.stem, strobilus, sterile leaf, fertile leaf, pinnule(s), rooting organ, rootlet, megasporangium, sporangium, spore, sterile axis, fertile axis, root
Slide PropertiesField to record types of prepared slides of specimens, noting the type of preparation and mounting medium, and to provide England Finder coordinates for palynomorph slides.strewn, petrographic thin-section, mounted peel
Geological Context IDAn identifier for the set of information associated with a GeologicalContext (the location within a geological context, such as stratigraphy). May be a global unique identifier or an identifier specific to the data set.https://opencontext.org/subjects/e54377f7-4452-4315-b676-40679b10c4d9geologicalContextID
